ASTER和SRTM，DEM对比

摘要
南安普顿大学，地理信息与环境专业，Topographic Data Analysis Techniques and Applications第二次作业

Difference between DEM derived from ASTER and downloaded from USGS’s SRTM

1. Introduction

In many geographical information systems, a digital elevation model (DEM) is one of the most essential spatial datasets (GIS). It’s a digital set of ground elevation (spot height) for landscape depiction that’s either ordered or unordered (Zhou, 2017). DEMs are a valuable data source for a variety of Earth and environmental science applications. The Earth’s topography is critical for everything from flood and runoff hydrologic modelling to assessing air boundary layer friction (Jing et al., 2013). DEM also contributes to the high integrity of Global Positioning System (GPS) pseudo-range measurement corrections, especially in environments with urban canyons (Wani et al., 2021).

The traditional approach to building the DEM is based on closed polygon traverses using total station traversing that begins and end at the same location on the ground surface. It is always used to convey the relatively small agricultural, forested and urban areas. A traverse is a group of line segments (traverse stations) with visible ends whose length and direction are established through observation. The surveyor picks a group of points placed at a topographic feature from each wire station (e.g., along a structure line). After a series of final tweaks and computations, DEM creation is accomplished (Florinsky, 2016). However, this method has some limitations. it cannot measure the place where the laser cannot reach. And it is difficult to measure surface elevation if any non-ground object exists in landslides detecting (Tsai et al., 2012). Or when the signal reaches the margins of an object, the accuracy may decrease. Because the reflected data comes from two separate locations, the coordinates will be calculated as an average, putting the point in the incorrect location (Mohammed, 2021).

Global Navigation Satellite Systems (GNSS) is another way to generate the DEM. It uses satellites such as GPS and GLONASS to get the position of ground points. A GNSS receiver picks up radio signals from a constellation of satellites. To calculate the coordinates (x, y, z) of a ground location, measurements from at least four satellites are required (Florinsky, 2016). The real-time kinematic’s (RTK) accuracy can reach± 10cm by using carrier phase measurements. It needs two receivers, the stationary reference station and the moving station. Via a transmitting communication link, the reference receiver calculates and broadcasts pseudo-range corrections (PCR) and range rate correction (RCC) in real-time, while a moving station receives these adjustments with a receiving communication link to compute exact placement. RTK is applied in airborne, land surveys, drones or other sensor data (Wani et al., 2021). The restricted satellite vision, multipath effect, interferences, and other limitations can all reduce GNSS’ positioning performance (Margaria et al., 2014).

In order to generate the DEM from the satellite images, it requires two or more images in the same area but from different directions which are called the stereo pairs (Jacobsen, 2003). From the stereo pairs, we reconstruct geometric parameters to calculate the all points position(x,y,z) by using the stereo correlation or image matching algorithm (Florinsky, 2016). Compared with other methods, the ground survey needs more effort and the laser scanning need expensive equipment, the GNSS can be influenced by the weather also need people to get there in person (Kayadibi, 2009). In that case, the satellite data source is a good choice in generating DEM with the lower price and easy to get the stereo image, such as the KFA-1000, SPOT, MOMS, IRS-1C/1D, ASTER, IKONOS, QuickBird and SRTM (Jacobsen, 2003).

2. Data and Methods

2.1 Study area

This study focuses on one region in America, the Rocky Mountains. The area is 3942 km2, elevation ranges from 1560 m to 3945 m, and covers approximately 62 by 62 km at 105° W and 38° N near. The mean elevation is 2534 m (Figure 2.1.1).

### 2.2 Data preparation * DEM extract from ASTER data using ENVI * SRTM DEM download from the USGS 1 arcsec/30 m

In 1999, an advanced multispectral imager the advanced spaceborne thermal emission and reflection radiometer (ASTER) is launched onboard from the terra spacecraft. Aster has 14 bands and is of high spatial, spectral and radiometric resolution. ASTER contains two telescopes, one for looking down at the ground and the other for looking backwards. The ASTER can generate two types of level-1 data, level-A and level-B. In this practice, we use the level-A data. Level-A data contains geometric parameters and spacecraft information, because of that, we don’t need to deal with the GCP in the processing (Fujisada et al., 2005).
For the Aster data, we use the ENVI classic 5.1 DEM extraction Wizard. Below is the brief process in that wizard. In defining the tie points, we choose automatic and then delete the error points to reduce the Y parallax under 10 pixels. ENVI will next compute the epipolar geometry and epipolar images that will be utilised to extract the DEM using the tie points. After specifying the DEM output projection and extraction parameters, we can get the output DEM.

Figure 2.2.1 ASTER DEM processing
The Shuttle Radar Topography Mission (SRTM) is a joint project between the National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency (NGA) and the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA). With data points every 1 arc-second, SRTM successfully gathered radar data across 80 per cent of the Earth's land surface between 60° north and 56° south latitude (EROS, 2018). The principle of the SRTM is interferometry which is researched from two sets of radar signals. The primary antennas in the Shuttle's payload compartment can both send and receive radar echoes, but the supplemental antennae at the end of a retractable 60-meter pole can only receive them. Accurate elevation of the Earth's surface may be computed using information about the distance between the two antennas and changes in the reflected radar wave signals.

The data we use is SRTM1(1 arc-second) whose accuracy is 30 m. in order to get the same area of the ASTER data, using ENVI to set the projection and save the image as tiff. After that, we can import the tiff into the ArcGIS pro and make a new polygon feature that covers the area on the map. Then export the feature to the shapefile. We can upload this file to USGS to get the study area and download the SRTM1 dem from the USGS. The SRTM we download is a satellite image covering our region, not the same as our region. We need to combine the image using ENVI mosaicking tools to clip our region from the whole image. Then Change the projection of ASTER data to WGS 84 which is the same as the SRTM data. After that, we can use the ArcGIS pro’s extract from mask tools, to extract the same region of ASTER dem from the SRTM data. Finally, because the spatial resolution of the DEM is not the same, the comparison should be at the same scale. To compare them, The ASTER DEM was scaled up to an aggregated pixel size that matched the SRTM grid’s proportions by using the resampling method in ArcGIS pro (Nikolakopoulos, 2006). ### 2.3 ASTER SRTM DEM comparison Now we have two DEMs of ASTER and SRTM1 in the same region and the same spatial resolution. Pearson and Spearman correlation coefficients were determined for the elevations as well as the slope obtained from them to determine the degree of connection between the ASTER and SRTM DEMs. Pearson's correlation coefficient denotes the relationship between two variables, or the degree of covariation between the two variables, or the tendency for two variables to vary together in the sense that one increases as the other increases (positive covariation), or one variable increases as the other decreases (negative covariation) (Nikolakopoulos, 2006). We calculate the correlation matrix about the DEM and their slope in this article.

Skewness and kurtosis were also used to compare their distribution. The skewness measures the asymmetry of the probability distribution of a random variable and is a measure of the degree of asymmetry with respect to the mean. The skewness of the normal distribution is 0, that is, the data distribution is symmetrical, and the skewness is 0. If the skewness is greater than positive, there is a long tail on the right; if the skewness is less than 0, it is negative, and there is a long tail on the left; at the same time, the greater the absolute value of the skewness, the more serious the deviation of the distribution. Kurtosis is a statistic that studies the steepness or smoothness of the data distribution. By measuring the kurtosis coefficient, we can determine whether the data is steeper or smoother relative to the normal distribution. The greater the kurtosis, the higher and the sharper the distribution is.

Furthermore, the root means square error (RMSE) is a widely used statistic for assessing vertical accuracy in Dems The root mean square error (RMSE) is a composite error statistic that accounts for both random and systematic mistakes that occur during data collection. The USGS use the RMSE technique to assess DEM results by comparing them to elevation points that represent the “most likely” elevation for a specific site. The RMSE of the SRTM DEM was determined in this study in comparison to a reference DEM (ASTER DEM) (Nikolakopoulos, 2006). In this article, the IDL is used to calculate the RMSE. Export the difference of DEM through the ArcGIS pro to simplify the process. Then compare their slope difference.

## 4. Conclusion The goal of this paper was to compare the difference of DEMs derived from the ASTER stereo image by ENVI wizard and SRTM DEM downloaded from the USGS website. In this article, we use ASTER DEM to clip the bigger SRTM DEM, so there is no displacement problem in this method.

The limitation of this method is in the correlation, the Pearson can be used to give more evidence. And in the slope part, a comparison between slope and curvature can make this method more dimensions.

The difference between two DEMs is produced by SRTM subtracting ASTER. The mean difference is 22m. the corresponding frequency histogram indicated that the ASTER elevation was underestimated. The comparison of SRTM and ASTER DEMs allowed for a qualitative evaluation of the horizontal and vertical components of the inaccuracy, while statistical techniques were utilized to determine vertical accuracy. We use skewness and kurtosis to compare the elevation distribution. The two DEMs has a similar shape regarding symmetry and fat-tailedness also in the slope distribution. The correlation of the elevation is 0.9 which indicates that the elevation of two DEMs is strongly correlated. The slope correlation is 0.4 which also shows the correlation between two DEMs’ slopes. For this reason, these two datasets have a strong correlation.

The aster DEM has an overall small range and is visually noticeably noisy. The slope map SRTM is smoother and has a smaller standard deviation. The RMSE value of the two DEMs is 3.89m, which is a good result, proving that the corresponding difference between the two DEMs is not large, indicating that although the ranges are offset, the overall trend of the same distribution.
