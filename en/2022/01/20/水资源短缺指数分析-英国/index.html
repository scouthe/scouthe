The water scarcity in The United Kingdom

1.Introduction

Water scarcity, defined as a lack of availability to acceptable quantities of water for human and environmental purposes, is rapidly being recognized as a severe and developing challenge in many countries (Chris, 2012). The population of the world, the life quality improvement and the irrigated agriculture expansion all request more water to the society in the past decade (Mekonnen, 2016).
The global hydrological cycle yearly provides several times fresher water than is required to support the world’s current population of approximately six billion (~6*109) people. But the distribution of most water is not average and cannot be used for us (Postel, 2000). In the future decades, water will be a major limitation for agriculture, necessitating major institutional changes, notably in Asia and Africa (Rijsberman, 2006).

A variety of indexes have been created during the last 20 years to quantify the sensitivity of water resources to resource fragility. The most common index is the Falkenmark index used to measure the level of water scarcity. They use the 1700 m3 of renewable water resources per year per capita to present the threshold, which is estimated the requirements of the household, agricultural, industrial and energy sectors (Rijsberman, 2006). An absolute water shortage occurs when the index threshold is less than 500 m3/person/year, and no stress occurs when the index threshold is larger than 1700 m3/person/year (Falkenmark, 1989).

Here are also two indices based on human water requirements. The Gleick define water scarcity by determining whether the water supply meets basic human needs, such as drinking water for survival, water for hygiene, sanitation services and household. All use the basic standard to get the 50 litres per person per day threshold (Gleick, 1996). Ohlsson construct a water scarcity index called Social Water Scarcity Index (SWSI). SWSI is calculated by using the first-generation waster scarcity index which measures the availability of renewable freshwater per capita to divide the Human Development Index (HDI). It can not only show the importance of the society’s adaptive ability facing water scarcity, but also avoid the anomalies value in the first-generation index (OhIsson, 2000).

Some people analyze water scarcity by the water footprinting. On a local or regional basis, water footprint (WF) indicators can quantify the amount of water utilised in a certain river basin or from an aquifer. Bluewater footprint is consumed by humans from blue water resources. And the volume of surface and groundwater consumed as a result of the creation of goods or services can be used to calculate the blue water footprint (Veettil, 2018). ‘ Green water footprint refers to the amount of water from precipitation that, after having been stored in the root zone of the soil (green water), is either lost by evapotranspiration or incorporated by plants. It is particularly relevant for agricultural, horticultural and forestry products.’ (Wikimedia Foundation, 2021) Agricultural, biofuel, and forestry products all have a green water footprint. The use of the water footprint idea is fast expanding in a variety of industries (Veettil, 2018).

2.Method

2.1 Study Area

The study area is the United Kingdom shown in figure 2.1. The total area is 93628 square miles (242500 km2), with an estimated population in 2020 of 68 million. Agriculture in the United Kingdom uses the country’s land area. Sheep provide most of the British wool, with only a few goats or alpacas cultivated for exotic wools like cashmere or angora. Cattle provide the bulk of milk, and hens provide most eggs.

2.2 Theory And Data prepare

The Falkenmark index is the widely used index. Because it is easy to get the relative data. However, its main limitations are obvious: (a) it fails to take enough environmental and social factors into account, thus it’s not convincing even as an indicator of physical scarcity; (b) the identification of the threshold is too arbitrary (Rijsberman, 2006). It depends on two variables: surface runoff (m3/yr) and population. But the surface runoff often has a large scale of spatial resolution which can not be compared with the high resolution of population. In that case, we use the criticality ratio – the ratio of water withdrawals for human use to total renewable water resources (Rijsberman, 2006). The population, livestock and irrigated construct the water consumption. The threshold of value is 0.4 commonly used to determine the boundary of water stress.

RI=(Population+livestock+irrigated)/(renewable water resource)

the renewable freshwater resources per capita in the United Kingdom is 2195 m3 (ourworldindata, 2017). The population counts layer comes from the world pop website and is 1km resolution with WGS84. The irrigation data is downloaded from the food and agriculture organization (FAO) website with 5 minutes resolution and the data is expressed in hectares per cell. And the livestock data is from the FAO livestock system with 5 minutes of arc spatial resolution. We choose cattle, sheep, chicken, and pig as livestock variables.

Gleick indicated that the human basis requirement of the water is 50 litres per person per day which is not influenced by the climate, technology, and culture (Gleick, 1996). From Knox, we know the total annual water use is 225 Mm3 (Knox, 2020) (616.4 m3 per day). The water consumption comes from Ward’s research. The average typical water requirement of cattle is 40 L/day, sheep is 6L/day, chicken is 300 L/1000 birds/day, and the pig is 9 L/day (Ward, 2007).

2.3 Methodology

For the first, we need to make them in the same projection. And as the maps we get are all the worldwide, we need to clip them into England region. Using the Extract by mask tool in ArcGIS pro.
Extract by mask extract the UK irrigation from the world. Because the resolution of livestock and irrigation is the same, we decide to make the population resolution the same as livestock resolution. In our method, we calculate the water consumption per person per day. We cannot use the resample tools, because that will change the population number in the region. Using the aggregate tool can generate the reduced-resolution raster image and the output cell can calculate the sum of the input cell that is contained in the output cell area.

Now we have the same resolution maps and we need to combine the livestock layers. Using the raster calculator to do that with the formula ‘“cattle” *40 + “chicken” *0.3 + “sheep” *6 + “pig” 9’. This is the four species’ water consumption per day. And the population layer multiplies 50 get the map layer of human consumption and multiply the renewable water 21951000/365 (per day) get the map layer of renewable water resource. Using irrigation to multiply the usage in irrigation gets the irrigation consumption map.

Next, we combine the three variable layers using the raster calculator. And get the final result RI. When the RI is under 20% means this place is rich in water, and 20% - 40% may have pressure in water. Over 60% means have severe water scarce (Brown, 2011). This is not the same as brown’s saying. Because my formula denominator is the renewable water and he is the available water which is not the same as the renewable water. The processing of this method is below (figure 2.2).

3.Result

Here is the result of the calculation. It shows that the north of the UK has severe water-scarce with a ratio is greater than 0.4. someplace the ratio is even greater than 1 which is not a good result. And in the south region, the most place is under the green colour, which means the water is adequate. Because the renewable data is calculated by population to multiply renewable water per capita, it makes the water’s distribution follow the population. In that case, in the north place where the people are less and the livestock and irrigation are much more, it will appear the ratio greater than 1. In the south region, the livestock and irrigation are less than in the north and the population is much more, so the water is rich in the south. The red place in the southwest corner is the area with the most livestock, but the population distribution is more scattered.

Figure 3.1 water scarcity index of criticality ratios
This histogram shows the statistic of water scarcity index, we can see that most pixels are less than 0.2 which means no pressure in water using. Only a few greater than the 0.4, and the pixels greater than 0.8 are much less. The mean is 0.1. From those we can know that the average of the United Kingdom water scarcity is under 0.2, rich in water. A few regions in the water-scarce.
Figure 3.2 Distribution of water scarcity index
Discussion For this method, we do not consider the distribution of the water on the geography side, because the data we have is the renewable water per capita, we just use this to multiply the population, which means more people, more water. It will lead to a result that the water distribution follows population distribution. The strength of this method is it does not consider the environmental impact on the water resource, which may make the calculation complexes. We just focus on the variables we input and how their relationship led to the result.

However, when this method avoids the different requirements in the different situations, it still has limitations:
Renewable water is based on the river and rainfall, not considered the man-made water supply (desalination or recycle water).
It ignores countries’ ability to adjust to reduced water supplies through behavioural changes or new technology (Chris, 2012).
The distribution of renewable water may not be the real distribution based on the population.
The data on water withdrawals do not account for how much of it is consumed (or evapotranspired) and how much is available for recycling through return flows (Damkjaer, 2017).

This method can be improved by changing the data source, such as using the renewable water distribution map, not just the number per capita. And add the industry water consumption to make this method better with different data types.
